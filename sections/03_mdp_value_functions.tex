\section{Three Big Ideas}

\begin{frame}{Three Influential Ideas in Offline Reinforcement Learning}
  Complementary approaches to improve behavior cloning:

  \vspace{0.35cm}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Reweight the logged data} \\
    \emph{Advantage-Weighted Regression (AWR):} copy successful decisions more strongly.

    \vspace{0.25cm}
    \item \textbf{Learn conservative value estimates} \\
    \emph{Conservative Q-Learning (CQL):} be pessimistic where evidence is weak.

    \vspace{0.25cm}
    \item \textbf{Estimate values only from supported actions} \\
    \emph{Implicit Q-Learning (IQL):} avoid maximizing over unsupported actions altogether.
  \end{enumerate}

  \note{This is the roadmap for the second half. Emphasize that the talk is now organized around ideas, not papers or algorithms.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Three Common RL Families}
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{ll}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Family} & \textbf{Basic idea} \\
    \midrule
    Value-based RL & Learn $Q(s,a)$, act greedily \\
    Policy gradient & Directly improve $\pi(a \mid s)$ \\
    Actor-critic & Learn both policy and value \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

  \vspace{0.4cm}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Q-learning is conceptually simple, but is most natural when actions are discrete.
    \item Policy gradients apply more generally, but require expensive rollouts.
    \item Actor-critic methods combine both ideas: they learn a policy and a value estimate, and work with discrete or continuous actions.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{Avoid a DQN/PPO survey. The goal is vocabulary and motivation for critics, actors, and value functions. Position actor-critic methods as a practical compromise: use value estimates for efficiency while retaining a policy representation that works for discrete and continuous actions.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Generic Actor-Critic Offline RL}
  Most modern actor-critic Offline RL methods separate two steps:

  \vspace{0.15cm}
  \[
  \begin{aligned}
    &\text{estimate } Q,V \\
    &\Updownarrow \\
    &\text{improve policy}
  \end{aligned}
  \]

  \vspace{0.25cm}
  The three ideas intervene at different points:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AWR} mainly changes the improve-policy step.
    \item \textbf{CQL} and \textbf{IQL} focus primarily on value estimation.
  \end{itemize}

  \vspace{0.25cm}
  Policy updates need values; methods differ in how those values are made reliable.
  \note{This is the conceptual anchor for the second half, but avoid implying that every method uses weighted behavior cloning. AWR is a policy-improvement method. CQL and IQL are primarily value-estimation philosophies.}
\end{frame}

\section{Idea 1: Reweight the Logged Data}

\begin{frame}{Problem: Not All Demonstrations Are Equally Useful}
  Behavior cloning treats every logged decision equally.

  \vspace{0.4cm}
  But logged data often contains a mixture:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item strong operator decisions,
    \item reasonable routine decisions,
    \item choices that led to delay, rehandling, or poor downstream outcomes.
  \end{itemize}

  \vspace{0.35cm}
  \begin{block}{Question}
    Can we learn more from the better demonstrations?
  \end{block}
  \note{This starts Chapter 1. Keep it as a reminder, not a full recap of BC.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Core Idea: Advantage}
  Compare the logged action to what is typical in the same state:
  \[
    A(s,a)=Q(s,a)-V(s).
  \]

  \vspace{0.2cm}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item $Q(s,a)$ measures how good it is to take action $a$ in state $s$.
    \item $V(s)$ measures how good the state is on average.
    \item Their difference says how much better this action is than expected.
  \end{itemize}

  \vspace{0.2cm}
  \centering
  \begin{tikzpicture}[x=1cm,y=0.8cm,>=Latex]
    \draw[->,Muted] (0,0) -- (8.4,0) node[right] {long-term value};
    \draw[thick,Muted] (3.8,-0.12) -- (3.8,0.12);
    \node[below,text=Muted] at (3.8,-0.12) {average action};

    \draw[fill=Panel] (3.25,0.75) rectangle (4.35,1.35);
    \node at (3.8,1.05) {$V(s)$};

    \draw[fill=SignalGreen!55] (6.0,0.75) rectangle (7.1,1.35);
    \node at (6.55,1.05) {$Q(s,a)$};
    \node[above,text=SignalGreen] at (6.55,1.43) {better than average};
    \draw[<->,thick,SignalGreen] (3.8,0.42) -- (6.55,0.42);
    \node[below,text=SignalGreen] at (5.18,0.38) {advantage};
  \end{tikzpicture}

  \vspace{0.15cm}
  This difference is called the \textbf{advantage}.
  \note{Define advantage before introducing advantage weights. Q measures the value of a particular action; V is the baseline value of the state. The advantage is the improvement over that baseline.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Advantage-Weighted Regression (AWR)}
  Behavior cloning solves:
  \[
    \min_\theta \sum_i \ell\!\left(\pi_\theta(s_i),a_i\right)
  \]

  \vspace{0.15cm}
  AWR keeps the same supervised-learning loss, but weights each example:
  \[
    \min_\theta
    \sum_i
    \textcolor{red}{\exp\!\left(\beta A(s_i,a_i)\right)}
    \,\ell\!\left(\pi_\theta(s_i),a_i\right).
  \]

  \vspace{0.25cm}
  \begin{block}{Key point}
    The only change is the weighting. Everything else remains supervised learning.
  \end{block}

  \vspace{0.15cm}
  \textbf{Next question:} Where do reliable estimates of $Q$ and $V$ come from?
  \note{This is the AWR slide. The audience should see that AWR is weighted behavior cloning: high-advantage logged decisions count more, but the policy update is still supervised learning. End by pointing to the next chapter: reliable value estimation.}
\end{frame}
