\section{First Obvious Idea: Imitate Good Operators}

\begin{frame}{Imitation Learning: Turn Decisions Into Supervised Learning}
  \large
  Given this situation, what would the operator usually do?

  \vspace{0.15cm}
  \normalsize
  \[
    \dataset = \{(s_t,a_t)\},
    \qquad
    \tau = (s_1,a_1,\ldots,s_T,a_T)
  \]
  \[
    \mathcal L_{\mathrm{BC}}(\theta)
    =
    -\mathbb E_{(s,a)\sim\dataset}
    \left[\log \pi_\theta(a\mid s)\right]
  \]

  \vspace{0.05cm}
  For deterministic continuous actions, use regression:
  \[
    \min_\theta\ \mathbb E\left[\|a-\pi_\theta(s)\|^2\right]
  \]

  \vspace{0.05cm}
  \productbox{Attractive because it is fully offline, simple to train, and does not require defining a reward function.}
  \note{This is the baseline everyone should think of first. Before asking for RL, ask whether behavior cloning is enough. It is just supervised learning on demonstrated decisions: given this situation, predict what the operator did.}
\end{frame}

  \vspace{0.28cm}
  \large
  Consider imitation data for self-driving cars:

  \vspace{0.08cm}
  \normalsize
  \begin{itemize}
    \item in similar observed states, driver 1 stays in lane
    \item driver 2 changes lanes
    \item squared-error regression learns the conditional mean action
  \end{itemize}

  \vspace{0.08cm}
  \textbf{Problem:} the mean action can lie between valid modes: a poor command that no expert demonstrated.
  \note{Use the driving image and action-density plot together. The training set can contain two valid behaviors for similar observed states: staying in lane and changing lanes. Squared-error regression treats these as noisy labels around one target and learns the mean. But the mean action may be a poor steering command that no driver demonstrated. This motivates richer policy distributions such as mixtures, autoregressive policies, or diffusion policies later.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Pitfall 2: Small Mistakes Compound}
  \large
  In supervised learning, errors do not change the next input.

  \vspace{0.15cm}
  \normalsize
  In behavior cloning, actions change future states:
  \[
    p_{\mathrm{expert}}(s) \neq p_{\pi}(s)
  \]

  \vspace{0.15cm}
  \centering
  \begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=0.3cm,>=Latex,scale=0.86,transform shape]
    \node[draw,rounded corners,fill=Panel,minimum width=2.0cm] (err1) {small error};
    \node[draw,rounded corners,fill=Panel,right=of err1,minimum width=2.2cm] (state) {new state};
    \node[draw,rounded corners,fill=Panel,right=of state,minimum width=2.0cm] (err2) {larger error};
    \node[draw,rounded corners,fill=Panel,right=of err2,minimum width=2.4cm] (ood) {OOD state};
    \node[draw,rounded corners,fill=Panel,right=of ood,minimum width=1.8cm] (fail) {failure};
    \draw[->,thick] (err1) -- (state);
    \draw[->,thick] (state) -- (err2);
    \draw[->,thick] (err2) -- (ood);
    \draw[->,thick] (ood) -- (fail);
  \end{tikzpicture}

  \vspace{0.2cm}
  One classical fix is DAgger: roll out the learned policy, ask the expert for corrections, and aggregate the new data.

  \vspace{0.08cm}
  \productbox{Offline logs often underrepresent recovery states, rare failures, and near-miss conditions.}

  \vspace{0.08cm}
  \textbf{Transition:} Behavior cloning asks what action was likely in the logs. RL asks which action led to better long-term outcomes.
  \note{Use a self-driving analogy: human demonstrations mostly show centered lane driving. A learned policy drifts slightly, then sees off-center states that are rare in the dataset, then errors compound. DAgger is the standard intervention-style fix: collect expert labels on states induced by the learned policy. This motivates RL as more than action prediction.}
\end{frame}
